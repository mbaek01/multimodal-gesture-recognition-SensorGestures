Interpretable Multimodal Gesture Recognition for Drone and Mobile Robot Teleoperation via Log-Likelihood Ratio Fusion

Wearable IMU and capacitive sensing for robust, efficient, and interpretable gesture-driven robot control.

Korea University · RPTU Kaiserslautern-Landau · DFKI

Overview

Sensor-based gesture recognition for real-world teleoperation

Abstract

Human operators are still frequently exposed to hazardous environments such as disaster zones and industrial facilities, where intuitive and reliable teleoperation of mobile robots and Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) is essential. In this context, hands-free teleoperation enhances operator mobility and situational awareness, thereby improving safety in hazardous environments. While vision-based gesture recognition has been explored as one method for hands-free teleoperation, its performance often deteriorates under occlusions, lighting variations, and cluttered backgrounds, limiting its applicability in real-world operations. To overcome these limitations, we propose a multimodal gesture recognition framework that integrates inertial data (accelerometer, gyroscope, and orientation) from Apple Watches on both wrists with capacitive sensing signals from custom gloves. We design a late fusion strategy based on the log-likelihood ratio (LLR), which not only enhances recognition performance but also provides interpretability by quantifying modality-specific contributions. To support this research, we introduce a new dataset of 20 distinct gestures inspired by aircraft marshalling signals, comprising synchronized RGB video, IMU, and capacitive sensor data. Experimental results demonstrate that our framework achieves performance comparable to a state-of-the-art vision-based baseline while significantly reducing computational cost, model size, and training time, making it well suited for real-time robot control. We therefore underscore the potential of sensor-based multimodal fusion as a robust and interpretable solution for gesture-driven mobile robot and drone teleoperation.

Key idea

Combine wrist IMU/orientation signals and capacitive glove signals through interpretable late fusion, so the system can classify gestures and show which modality contributed to each prediction. Use wearable sensors as a robust, vision-independent control channel for gesture-driven robot teleoperation.

  • Background: mobile robot and drone teleoperation is important in hazardous environments where operators need hands-free control.
  • Vision-based gesture recognition can degrade under occlusion, poor lighting, smoke, and cluttered backgrounds.
  • LLR fusion combines wrist IMU/orientation and capacitive glove signals while preserving modality-level evidence.
  • The sensor-based LLR fusion model matches the gesture recognition performance of the vision-based PoseConv3D baseline.
  • Compared with the video baseline, LLR fusion uses less compute, a smaller model, and faster training.

Dataset & Hardware

Multimodal capture with wearable sensors and RGB video

Dataset and capture setup

We record synchronized wearable-sensor and RGB video data for 20 aircraft-marshalling-inspired commands for drone and mobile robot teleoperation. The glove hardware builds on the CaptAinGlove textile capacitive-and-inertial glove design for real-time drone-control gestures.

ItemDetails
Gestures20 gesture classes selected for clear hand and arm motions inspired by aircraft marshalling signals.
Participants11 participants recorded over five sessions, with four randomized repetitions of each gesture per session.
Wrist sensorsApple Watch Series 7 devices on both wrists provide accelerometer, gyroscope, and quaternion orientation streams at approximately 100 Hz.
Glove sensorsTextile sensing gloves provide four capacitive channels per hand through an FDC2214 converter at approximately 50 Hz, together with glove IMU signals.
VideoA ZED Mini camera records synchronized RGB video at 30 fps for baseline comparison and visualization.

Method

Per-modality encoders with interpretable late fusion

Each left- and right-hand sensor stream is encoded independently, then fused to predict the gesture label while retaining modality-level evidence.

1. Encode each modality

Each sensor stream is processed independently to extract local and temporal gesture features.

2. Fuse predictions

LLR fusion combines class evidence from each modality while preserving modality-specific contribution scores.

3. Explain decisions

Visualize LLR contribution and attention patterns to diagnose which signals drove a prediction.
